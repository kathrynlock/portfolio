My phone holds infinite scratch space, and yet the best ideas I have had this semester live in a 4×6 Field Notes book in my backpack. There is something about the friction of a pen that lets a thought finish itself.

I have tried every digital notes app that exists. I have had a good run with several of them. The problem is not the apps — the problem is that my fingers already know how to type, and so typing never slows me down enough to notice whether what I am writing is actually worth writing.

A pen is different. It costs effort. That effort turns out to be the filter I needed.

The other thing about paper is that it is bad at search and good at stumbling. When I flip back through a notebook looking for a specific thing, I find three things I had completely forgotten. Serendipity by design. A digital search gives you exactly what you ask for, which is only useful if you knew what you were looking for.

I keep a separate notebook for project notes, one for personal journaling, and one that is just a place to put things that do not fit anywhere else — clippings, receipts, weird observations. The notebook that does not fit a category always has the most interesting stuff in it.

None of this is an argument against digital tools. It is an argument for using paper for the things paper is good at, which is: slow thinking, half-formed ideas, and anything you want to remember in a way that doesn't require charging a device.
