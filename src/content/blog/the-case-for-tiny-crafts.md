I started a 3-inch embroidery hoop two weeks ago and finished it last night, which is the fastest I have finished anything creative in months. There is a thesis in there about scope.

The thesis is this: large creative projects carry their ambition as overhead. Every time you sit down to work on something big, you have to first re-convince yourself that it is worth doing. Small projects sidestep this entirely. You can finish one before the doubt arrives.

I have a theory that the creative block most people experience is not actually a block — it is a misalignment between project size and available energy. On a good day with three free hours, a big project makes sense. On a Wednesday night with forty-five minutes, you need something you can actually finish.

Tiny crafts solve for Wednesday nights.

The other thing I have noticed is that tiny projects accumulate into something. A shelf of completed 3-inch embroidery hoops is more satisfying than a single unfinished large piece. The shelf is evidence. It says: I kept showing up.

There is also something to the constraint of size itself. Working small forces decisions. You cannot include everything, so you have to choose what matters. This turns out to be good practice for all the other kinds of choosing.

My current small-project lineup: a miniature zine about the best sandwiches in Austin, an embroidery of my dorm window view, and a terrarium that requires watering once a week and asking nothing else of me. All of them fit in my backpack. All of them are, in some small way, done.
