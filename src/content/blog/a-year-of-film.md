In January I put my digital camera in a drawer and made myself a rule: only film for one year. I expected to learn about light. I ended up learning about waiting, which turns out to be the actual skill.

The first roll was a disaster. I had forgotten about the exposure triangle in a way that only becomes apparent when you cannot immediately review what you just shot. I overexposed half a beach day and underexposed an entire birthday party. The lab sent them back in an envelope that felt more like a verdict than a delivery.

By roll three I had slowed down. The limited frame count does something to your attention — 36 shots is not that many, and you start to feel the weight of each one. I noticed I was waiting longer before pressing the shutter. Looking at things. Deciding.

The CVS near my apartment still processes film, which I did not know until I walked in holding a canister and the person at the counter knew exactly what to do. She told me they get maybe two or three film customers a week now, down from fifty a day in 2002. I found this both sad and strangely encouraging.

What I did not expect was the social dimension. People ask about the camera in a way they never ask about a phone. It starts conversations. A Pentax K1000 turns out to be a social object in a way that an iPhone is not, even though the iPhone takes objectively better photos.

The year ended with twelve rolls and a better understanding of light than I started with. I also have a box of prints that feel more real to me than anything in my camera roll — not because they are better, but because there is only one of each of them.
