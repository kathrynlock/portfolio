I closed WithLoveKKate this spring after four years. I want to write about it before the details fade — the packing-tape muscle memory, the post office friendships, the time a custom order went missing in Vermont.

I opened the shop my sophomore year of high school with $40 in supplies and a very optimistic grasp of profit margins. The first sale came three weeks after launch, to a stranger in Ohio, who left a five-star review that I printed out and kept. I still have it somewhere.

What I did not understand at the beginning was that running a shop is mostly logistics. It is inventory and packaging and postage and responding to messages at 11pm because a customer is stressed about a holiday deadline. The making is the good part. The making is maybe 20% of it.

The post office on Manchaca became a regular stop. The clerks knew my name. One of them, a woman who had been working there for twelve years, would sometimes have the postage printed before I even set the packages down. That kind of regularity felt like something.

The custom order that went missing in Vermont: I had made a hand-lettered piece for someone's anniversary. It went missing for six weeks, turned up at the wrong address, and then disappeared again. The customer was incredibly gracious about it. I made a replacement, absorbed the cost, and learned that tracking numbers are not guarantees.

What I learned that I will keep: how to price things honestly, how to write a product description that does actual work, how to handle a complaint without taking it personally, and that people who buy handmade things are, on average, very kind.

I closed the shop because I was starting college and could not do both well. The grief was real and a little embarrassing. It was a thing I had built, and then I stopped building it. But I think that is also fine. Not everything needs to run forever.
